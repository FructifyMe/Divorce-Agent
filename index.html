The two paths

1A · Uncontested

You agree on everything

Both spouses agree the marriage is over and agree on the terms (parenting, support, property). Filed jointly with a signed Separation Agreement. The faster, calmer, less expensive route — roughly 4–6 months.

1B · Contested

Terms aren't settled (yet)

One spouse wants the divorce, or you don't yet agree on the terms. Massachusetts builds in a waiting period (no judgment sooner than 6 months after filing). Many 1B cases settle into a 1A once terms are worked out.

Your journey at a glance

1. Get organized — understand the process, gather documents, build your financial picture.
2. Choose your path — 1A or 1B, decided with your attorney.
3. File & the freeze — pay the fee; an Automatic Restraining Order protects assets.
4. Exchange finances — both file a sworn Financial Statement and share documents.
5. Parenting course — if minor children, the online "Two Families Now" course.
6. Work out terms — parenting, support, property — by negotiation, mediation, or court.
7. Finalize + wait — a judge approves; ~90–120 days later it's final.

Process & Filing

How a Massachusetts divorce actually moves, step by step.

Can you file here? (Residency)

Generally yes if you've lived in Massachusetts for one year, or the breakdown happened here while you lived here as a couple. Venue (county) is usually where you last lived together if one spouse still lives there.

1A steps
  1. Confirm you can file in MA
  2. Negotiate & sign the Separation Agreement (notarized)
  3. File Joint Petition + agreement + fee
  4. Brief hearing — judge reviews fairness
  5. Judgment "nisi" → final ~120 days later
1B steps
  1. File the Complaint + fee
  2. Serve your spouse (the freeze begins)
  3. Exchange financials (Rule 410 / 401)
  4. Parenting course if minor kids
  5. Negotiate / temporary orders / pretrial
  6. Settle or go to trial → judgment → final 90 days later

The "Automatic Restraining Order" (Rule 411)

Not about safety — it's a financial freeze that starts automatically when a case is filed. While the case is pending, neither spouse may sell/hide/transfer marital assets (beyond ordinary expenses), run up unreasonable debt, or drop anyone from insurance or change beneficiaries. It keeps things fair.

Parenting course — "Two Families Now"

If you have minor children and don't already have a parenting agreement, both parents take a 4-hour online course ($49 each, fee can be waived). Register within 30 days of service; finish within 30 days of registering.

Money & Property

How Massachusetts divides what you own and owe.

The big idea

Equitable ≠ equal

MA divides property equitably (fairly), which isn't always 50/50. A judge weighs many factors: length of marriage, each spouse's income/health/contributions (including as a homemaker), and future needs.

What's included

Almost everything is "in the pot"

Regardless of whose name it's in or when you got it — including, in many cases, inheritances, gifts, and premarital assets. Whether those get split depends on marriage length and whether they were mixed into shared finances. A professional degree/license is not divisible property.

The home

Options for the house

  • Sell & split the equity
  • Buyout — one keeps it, compensates the other
  • Defer the sale — sometimes until kids finish school
Retirement

401(k)s, pensions & QDROs

Splitting a retirement account usually needs a special court order called a QDRO. Done right, money moves between spouses tax- and penalty-free. Your attorney handles the order.

Debt

Dividing what you owe

Debts are split fairly by who ran them up and why. Watch out: if your agreement says your ex pays a joint debt but they don't, the lender can still come after you. Best fix: pay off, refinance, or remove your name.

Taxes

After-tax value matters

$100k in savings ≠ $100k in a 401(k) (taxed later) ≠ $100k of appreciated stock. Always compare after-tax value — a CPA is worth it. Spousal transfers in divorce are generally tax-free, but carry the original cost basis.

Disclosure

Full, honest disclosure

Both spouses file a sworn Financial Statement and exchange documents. Because so much is divisible, a complete, accurate picture is everything — hiding assets can reopen a judgment.

Complex assets

Businesses & stock options

A business is valued by an appraiser; one spouse often keeps it and offsets the other. Stock options/RSUs earned during the marriage are typically divisible using a time-based formula.

Support — Alimony & Child Support

Two kinds of support, and they interact. Your attorney runs the actual numbers.

Alimony — four types

General term

Ongoing support when one spouse earns much more — most common in longer marriages.

Rehabilitative

Temporary, while the receiving spouse gets back on their feet (training, re-entering work).

Reimbursement

Short marriages (≤5 yrs) — pays someone back for, e.g., funding a spouse's education.

Transitional

Short marriages (≤5 yrs) — helps one spouse adjust to a new situation or location.

How long can general-term alimony last?

Length of marriageMaximum duration
5 years or lessUp to 50% of the months married
Over 5 to 10 yearsUp to 60%
Over 10 to 15 yearsUp to 70%
Over 15 to 20 yearsUp to 80%
Over 20 yearsIndefinite (judge's discretion)

How much? Generally not more than the recipient's need or 30–35% of the difference between the spouses' incomes. It ends on remarriage, 3+ months of cohabitation, or the payer's full retirement age.

Child support — 2025 Guidelines

Set by an official state worksheet (effective Dec 1, 2025). Based on both incomes, parenting time, the children's health-insurance cost, and work-related child care. Income is considered up to a $450,000 combined cap.

  • Generally runs to 18; can continue to 21 (if living with a parent and dependent) or 23 (if also in an undergraduate program).
  • College costs can be ordered but are discretionary, with a cap tied to UMass-Amherst's in-state cost.
  • Orders can be modified on a significant change — or if the order no longer matches the current guidelines.
💡 Older online calculators may use pre-2025 numbers. Ask your attorney to run the current official worksheet.

Custody & Kids

In Massachusetts, custody decisions follow one standard above all: the best interests of the child.

Decision-making

Legal custody

Who makes major decisions — school, medical care, religion. Can be sole (one parent) or shared (both, jointly). Shared legal custody is common.

Where they live

Physical custody

Where the children primarily live and the day-to-day parenting time. Can be sole or shared. "Shared" doesn't have to mean exactly 50/50.

The parenting plan

A written schedule covering the regular week, holidays, school vacations, summers, transportation/exchanges, and how parents communicate and make decisions. Courts strongly favor detailed, child-focused plans parents build themselves over ones a judge has to impose.

Best-interest factors

A judge weighs stability, each parent's relationship with the child, the child's needs and adjustment (home/school/community), each parent's ability to cooperate, and any history of abuse or neglect.

Guardian ad Litem (GAL)

In contested custody cases a court may appoint a GAL — a neutral investigator who interviews the family and recommends what serves the child. Their report carries weight.

Relocation ("removal")

Moving a child out of Massachusetts generally needs the other parent's consent or court permission. Don't move first and ask later.

Parenting course

Parents of minor children take the 4-hour online "Two Families Now" course ($49 each). Required in most cases without an agreement.

Co-parenting that works

  • Keep communication businesslike and child-focused — many parents use a shared app or single email thread.
  • Never use the children as messengers or put the other parent down in front of them.
  • Keep a simple shared record of child expenses to split (the Toolkit can help).
  • Consistency between homes — bedtimes, rules, routines — helps kids feel secure.
💛 Kids do best when conflict between parents stays low. That's a goal worth protecting, even when it's hard.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a divorce take?

Uncontested (1A) often 4–6 months; contested (1B) 12–18 months. Even after approval there's a ~90–120 day waiting period before it's final.

How much does it cost to file?

About $215 (1A) or $220 (1B). If money is tight, an Affidavit of Indigency can waive court fees. Attorney costs are separate.

Do I need a lawyer?

Not required, but divorce involves binding decisions — at minimum, have an attorney review your agreement.

Where will my case be heard?

Essex County → Salem, or Lawrence if either spouse lives in Methuen, Lawrence, Andover, North Andover, or Haverhill. No Newburyport divorce court.

Can I file if my spouse won't agree?

Yes — Massachusetts has no-fault divorce. If they don't agree, it's a contested (1B) case with a built-in waiting period.

Will I get half of everything?

Not necessarily. MA divides property equitably (fairly), which isn't always equally.

Is my inheritance protected?

Maybe. In MA almost everything can be considered, though inheritances and premarital assets are often kept with the original owner depending on marriage length and whether they were mixed into shared finances.

What happens to the house?

Common options: sell and split, one spouse buys out the other, or delay the sale (sometimes until kids finish school).

What is the "automatic restraining order"?

A financial freeze on filing — it stops either spouse from selling off/hiding assets or dropping insurance while the case is pending. It's not about physical safety.

How is child support calculated?

An official state worksheet based on both incomes, parenting time, health insurance, and child care. The guidelines were updated Dec 1, 2025.

Can support or custody change later?

Yes, on a significant change in circumstances. Child support can also be revisited if it no longer matches current guidelines.

Can I remarry right away?

No — only after the divorce becomes final (absolute), following the post-judgment waiting period.

What's the difference between legal and physical custody?

Legal custody is decision-making (school, medical, religion); physical custody is where the child lives. Each can be sole or shared.

What should I bring to my first attorney meeting?

Your financial picture (income, assets, debts), any prenup, prior court orders, and a written list of priorities and questions. Your toolkit and expense sheet are built for exactly this.

Glossary — divorce terms in plain English

The words you'll hear from lawyers and the court, decoded.

1A divorce

An uncontested, no-fault divorce filed jointly when both spouses agree it's over and agree on all terms.

1B divorce

A contested no-fault divorce — filed when one spouse wants it or the terms aren't settled.

No-fault / irretrievable breakdown

The legal ground that the marriage has broken down with no reasonable chance of reconciliation. No one has to prove wrongdoing.

Separation Agreement

The written contract settling parenting, support, and property. In a 1A it's filed with the petition and reviewed by the judge.

Merge vs. survive

Whether terms of your agreement can be changed later (merge) or stand as an unchangeable contract (survive). An important attorney-level choice.

Judgment nisi

The "almost final" divorce judgment. After it enters, a waiting period runs before the divorce becomes absolute.

Absolute

The divorce is fully final. Only then can you remarry.

Automatic Restraining Order (Rule 411)

The financial freeze that starts on filing — no moving/hiding assets or dropping insurance while pending.

Financial Statement

A sworn court form listing income, expenses, assets, and debts. Short Form under $75k income; Long Form at or above.

Discovery

The formal exchange of information and documents between spouses before settlement or trial.

Equitable distribution

Dividing marital property fairly — which is not always equally.

Marital estate

Everything potentially subject to division — in MA, broad, and can include premarital and inherited assets.

QDRO

Qualified Domestic Relations Order — the special order needed to split a retirement plan tax-free.

Alimony

Support paid from one spouse to the other. Four types in MA: general, rehabilitative, reimbursement, transitional.

Child support guidelines

The state formula (2025 version) that sets the presumptive child support amount.

Legal vs. physical custody

Decision-making authority vs. where the child lives. Each can be sole or shared.

Parenting plan

The detailed schedule and rules for how separated parents raise their children.

Guardian ad Litem (GAL)

A neutral appointed to investigate and recommend what's best for the child in a contested case.

Pretrial conference

A court meeting (in contested cases) to narrow issues and push toward settlement before any trial.

Temporary orders

Short-term rulings on support, custody, or use of the home while the case is pending.

Mediation

A neutral third party helps both spouses reach agreement — often faster and cheaper than litigating.

Pro se

Representing yourself without a lawyer.

Affidavit of Indigency

A request to waive court fees if you can't afford them.

Service of process

Formally delivering the divorce papers to your spouse so the case can proceed.

Get Ready — smart moves & mistakes to avoid

Preparation is the part you control — and it genuinely affects cost, speed, and stress.

✓ Smart moves
  • Gather documents early and keep them organized in one place.
  • Build a complete, honest financial picture (assets, debts, income, expenses).
  • Get clear on your top 2–3 priorities so you don't fight over everything.
  • Keep a running question list for your attorney; arrive prepared.
  • Keep communication with your spouse calm and in writing.
  • Look after yourself — sleep, support network, professional help if needed.
✕ Mistakes to avoid
  • Hiding or moving assets — it can reopen the judgment and destroy credibility.
  • Draining joint accounts or running up debt once a case is filed (Rule 411).
  • Venting about your spouse to or in front of the kids.
  • Posting about the divorce on social media.
  • Agreeing to terms you don't understand just to be done.
  • Moving the children out of state without consent or court permission.

Questions worth asking your attorney

  • Based on my facts, is 1A or 1B the realistic path?
  • How is our marital estate likely to be viewed — and what about an inheritance or premarital asset?
  • Should our agreement terms merge or survive?
  • What does the child support worksheet produce for us, and is alimony likely?
  • What's my realistic timeline and total cost estimate?
